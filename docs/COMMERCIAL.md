# Colomin Commercial Terms (Draft)

This document describes the planned commercial terms for official business use of Colomin.

## 1. Scope

These terms apply to companies, organizations, and teams using Colomin in production or internal business operations when they require official binaries, branding rights, support, or compliance assurances.

## 2. Free Community Use

Individual users, hobby projects, students, and non-commercial personal use can use the open-source edition for free under the project open-source license.

## 3. Open-Source Code License

Project source code is intended to be offered under AGPL v3 (or later).

Implications:
- You can study, run, and modify the source code.
- If you distribute modified versions, you must provide source code under AGPL terms.
- If you provide the software over a network as a service, AGPL obligations apply.

## 4. Official Business Offering

A paid commercial agreement covers official business usage features, which may include:
- Official signed binaries and installers
- Rights to use official Colomin branding in business deployment
- Priority support and issue handling SLAs
- Security and maintenance update commitments
- Optional enterprise features and compliance artifacts

## 5. Restrictions Without Commercial Agreement

Without a commercial agreement, organizations may not:
- Represent a fork/build as the official Colomin product
- Use Colomin trademarks/logos as if they are the official release
- Claim official support, certification, or warranty

## 6. Pricing and Plans

Pricing is defined in separate plan schedules and may vary by:
- Team size
- Support level
- Deployment type
- Compliance requirements

## 7. Warranty and Liability

Open-source/community use is provided as-is, without warranty.
Commercial agreements may define limited warranties and liability caps.

## 8. Contact

For commercial licensing and official business terms, contact the Colomin maintainer.

## 9. Status

This is a policy draft for repository guidance and is not legal advice.
Final legal language should be reviewed by legal counsel before broad commercial rollout.
